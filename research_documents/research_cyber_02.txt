Ransomware operations have evolved into sophisticated double-extortion campaigns where threat actors exfiltrate sensitive data before encrypting victim systems, threatening public disclosure to compel payment. Initial access vectors typically exploit unpatched CVEs in internet-facing appliances such as VPN concentrators, Microsoft Exchange servers, and Citrix ADC gateways. Phishing campaigns delivering weaponized Office macros, ISO disk images, and OneNote attachments establish persistence through scheduled tasks, WMI event subscriptions, and registry run keys. Post-exploitation frameworks including Cobalt Strike beacons, Sliver implants, and Brute Ratel C4 enable command-and-control communications over encrypted HTTPS channels with domain fronting and malleable C2 profiles. Lateral movement leverages credential dumping via Mimikatz, pass-the-hash attacks against NTLM authentication, and Kerberoasting of service principal names to escalate domain privileges. Ransomware payloads employ AES-256 symmetric encryption with RSA-4096 wrapped keys, making decryption without the private key computationally infeasible. Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) ecosystems like LockBit, BlackCat/ALPHV, and Royal operate affiliate programs with revenue-sharing models, lowering the barrier to entry for cybercriminals. Indicators of compromise (IOCs) including file hashes, mutex names, and command-and-control IP addresses are shared through STIX/TAXII threat intelligence feeds. Organizations deploy immutable backup strategies with air-gapped storage and WORM compliance to ensure recovery without ransom payment.
