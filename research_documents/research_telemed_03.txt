Healthcare policy frameworks governing telemedicine practice must address interstate medical licensure, prescribing authority, malpractice liability, and reimbursement parity to enable sustainable virtual care delivery at scale. The Interstate Medical Licensure Compact allows physicians to obtain expedited licenses in participating member states, reducing administrative barriers to cross-border teleconsultation. The Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act requires at least one in-person evaluation before prescribing controlled substances via telemedicine, though DEA pandemic-era flexibilities temporarily waived this requirement. Malpractice insurance carriers have developed telemedicine-specific endorsements addressing jurisdictional liability questions when physicians treat patients across state boundaries. The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) Connected Care Pilot Program and USDA ReConnect Program fund broadband infrastructure deployment in underserved rural communities to eliminate connectivity barriers to telehealth access. Digital health literacy interventions using community health workers and patient navigators improve technology adoption rates among elderly and low-income populations. Mobile health (mHealth) applications must comply with FDA medical device regulations when they transform smartphone sensors into diagnostic instruments, requiring 510(k) premarket clearance for Class II devices. Telestroke networks connect rural emergency departments with board-certified vascular neurologists through high-definition video consultation, enabling administration of tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) within the critical 4.5-hour treatment window. Health equity metrics track telemedicine utilization rates stratified by race, age, income, and geography to identify and address disparities in virtual care access.
