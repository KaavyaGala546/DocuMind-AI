Quantum computing fundamentally exploits superposition and entanglement of qubits to achieve computational speedups over classical binary architectures. Unlike classical bits restricted to 0 or 1, qubits exist in coherent superpositions, enabling massively parallel quantum gate operations. Quantum circuits built from Hadamard gates, CNOT gates, and Toffoli gates manipulate qubit registers to implement algorithms like Grover's search and quantum phase estimation. The computational advantage arises from quantum interference patterns that amplify correct solution amplitudes while destructively cancelling incorrect paths. Entanglement between qubit pairs creates non-local correlations described by Bell states, enabling quantum teleportation and superdense coding protocols. Current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) processors from IBM, Google, and IonQ operate with 50-1000 physical qubits, though quantum error correction requires thousands of physical qubits per logical qubit. The surface code and color code are leading error correction schemes that use topological properties to protect quantum information from decoherence. Quantum volume and circuit depth metrics benchmark processor performance beyond raw qubit counts. Variational quantum eigensolvers (VQE) and quantum approximate optimization algorithms (QAOA) represent hybrid classical-quantum approaches suited for near-term hardware constraints.
