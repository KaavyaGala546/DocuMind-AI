Quantum decoherence remains the dominant obstacle to fault-tolerant quantum computation, causing qubits to lose quantum coherence through interactions with thermal phonons and electromagnetic fluctuations in the processor environment. T1 relaxation time measures energy decay from excited to ground state, while T2 dephasing time quantifies the loss of phase coherence between superposition components. Superconducting transmon qubits achieve T1 times of 100-300 microseconds at millikelvin temperatures maintained by dilution refrigerators operating near 15 millikelvin. Trapped-ion architectures using ytterbium-171 and calcium-43 ions demonstrate coherence times exceeding several seconds, offering superior qubit fidelity at the cost of slower gate speeds. Topological qubits based on Majorana fermions in semiconductor-superconductor heterostructures promise inherent protection against local noise through non-abelian braiding operations, though experimental realization remains challenging. Quantum error correction codes such as the Steane code, Shor's 9-qubit code, and the surface code encode logical qubits across multiple physical qubits, using syndrome measurements to detect and correct bit-flip and phase-flip errors without collapsing the quantum state. The threshold theorem guarantees that arbitrarily long quantum computations are possible provided the physical error rate falls below a critical threshold, typically around 1% for surface codes. Cross-resonance and Molmer-Sorensen entangling gates achieve two-qubit gate fidelities above 99.5% in state-of-the-art processors.
